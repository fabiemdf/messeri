Understanding the Insurance Appraisal Process

February 10, 2025
David Messeri
Claims Process

When you and your insurance company disagree on a claim value, the appraisal process can help resolve the dispute without the need for costly litigation. As WIND Certified Appraisers with extensive experience in Florida insurance claims, we've guided countless property owners through this important process.

"The appraisal process offers property owners a structured path to resolve valuation disputes without resorting to expensive and time-consuming litigation."

What is Insurance Appraisal?

Insurance appraisal is a formal dispute resolution method included in most property insurance policies. It provides a way to resolve disagreements about the amount of loss when you and your insurance company cannot reach an agreement. Unlike litigation, the appraisal process focuses solely on the value of damages, not on coverage issues.

When Should You Invoke Appraisal?

Consider the appraisal process when:

  • Your insurance company's damage estimate is significantly lower than independent estimates
  • The insurance adjuster overlooked substantial damages
  • Negotiations have stalled and settlement offers remain inadequate
  • You want to avoid the time and expense of litigation

The Key Players in the Appraisal Process

The appraisal involves three main parties:

  1. Your Appraiser: You select and hire this professional to represent your interests
  2. Insurer's Appraiser: Selected by your insurance company
  3. The Umpire: A neutral third party jointly selected by both appraisers (or appointed by a court if the appraisers cannot agree)

The Appraisal Process: Step by Step

  1. Initiating Appraisal: Either you or your insurance company can formally invoke the appraisal provision by sending written notice.
  2. Selecting Appraisers: Both parties select their own qualified appraiser. Your appraiser should have experience in damage assessment and construction costs.
  3. Choosing an Umpire: The two appraisers select a neutral umpire. If they cannot agree, either party can petition the court to appoint one.
  4. Damage Assessment: Each appraiser independently evaluates the damage to your property and prepares a detailed estimate.
  5. Appraisal Meeting: The appraisers meet to compare their findings and attempt to resolve differences. The umpire gets involved only for items where the appraisers cannot agree.
  6. Award Determination: The final award is determined when any two of the three participants (your appraiser, the insurer's appraiser, or the umpire) agree on the amount of loss.
  7. Payment: Once the award is signed, your insurance company must pay the determined amount, minus any applicable deductible.

The Advantages of Appraisal

  • Efficiency: Typically faster than litigation, often concluding within 60-90 days
  • Cost-Effectiveness: Generally less expensive than going to court
  • Expert Evaluation: Properties are assessed by professionals with industry expertise
  • Binding Resolution: The appraisal award is binding on both parties
  • Preservation of Relationships: Less adversarial than litigation

Potential Challenges to Be Aware Of

  • Appraiser Selection: The expertise and negotiation skills of your appraiser greatly impact the outcome
  • Scope Disputes: Disagreements about what damages should be included in the appraisal
  • Policy Interpretation: Differences in interpreting policy language can complicate the process
  • Costs: You're responsible for your appraiser's fee and half of the umpire's fee

Preparing for the Appraisal Process

Successful appraisals require thorough preparation:

  • Gather all claim-related documents, including the policy and correspondence
  • Document all damages with detailed photos and videos
  • Obtain independent repair estimates from qualified contractors
  • Track all expenses related to the damage
  • Maintain a communication log with your insurance company

Is Appraisal Right for Your Claim?

While appraisal can be an effective way to resolve valuation disputes, it's not appropriate for every situation. Coverage disputes, for example, cannot be resolved through appraisal. A qualified public adjuster can help you determine if appraisal is the right path for your specific claim.
